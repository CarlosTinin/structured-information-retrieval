\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a multi-layer legal NLP framework for extracting structured information from Brazilian criminal labor-law judicial decisions related to modern slavery. Starting from a heterogeneous corpus (2,581 documents), the framework applies: (i) document-type filtering to retain only final decisions, (ii) legal-text normalization with task-specific outputs for classification and NER, (iii) penal-merit classification using Legal-BERT embeddings with classical classifiers, (iv) LLM-assisted sentence segmentation for conviction-focused documents, and (v) legal-domain NER for entity extraction. We describe the full pipeline, evaluation protocol, and error analysis, and discuss practical deployment constraints, reproducibility, and ethical/legal considerations. Results indicate that layered filtering and task-oriented preprocessing substantially improve downstream extraction quality in low-resource legal settings.
\end{abstract}

\begin{highlights}
\item Multi-layer filtering for noisy, heterogeneous legal corpora.
\item Task-specific normalization improves classification and NER compatibility.
\item Conviction-focused segmentation and legal-domain NER for structured extraction.
\item Reproducible benchmark design for Portuguese criminal labor-law decisions.
\end{highlights}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

The proposed pipeline spans four distinct technical areas: legal document classification and filtering, sentence segmentation and structural analysis of legal texts, named entity recognition in legal and low-resource language settings, and end-to-end legal NLP frameworks. This section surveys the most relevant prior work in each area, identifying the key contributions and limitations that motivate the design choices made in the present study.

\subsection{Legal document filtering and classification}

The ability to distinguish legally relevant documents from heterogeneous corpora is a prerequisite for any downstream extraction task. Ariai et al.~[02\_02] provide a comprehensive survey of NLP tasks in the legal domain, systematically characterizing the unique challenges that complicate classification: extensive document lengths, highly specialized vocabulary, jurisdiction-dependent terminology, and the scarcity of openly available annotated datasets. The authors identify Legal Text Classification (LTC) as a well-studied but unevenly distributed subfield, with a strong concentration of work on Chinese and English legal systems and comparatively little attention to other languages and jurisdictions. Their identification of domain-specific fine-tuning as a critical success factor directly informs the use of Portuguese Legal-BERT and BERTimbau encoders in Stage2 of the present work.

Resck et al.~[02\_01] demonstrate the effectiveness of combining a transformer-based classifier with an interpretability mechanism for the identification of implicit citation relationships in Brazilian Supreme Court decisions. Although their task differs from multi-class merit classification, their architecture illustrates the viability of applying machine learning classification to large-scale Brazilian legal corpora — precisely the linguistic and institutional context addressed in the present study. The visual analytics layer they develop also highlights the practical importance of explainability in legal AI systems. Nuranti et al.~[02\_03] contribute a systematic, reproducible pipeline for corpus construction and NER annotation covering Indonesian judicial decisions across three hierarchical court levels. Their use of BIO tagging with more than 50 entity types, combined with Fleiss' Kappa-based inter-annotator agreement evaluation, establishes methodological best practices that inform the annotation and quality-control protocol adopted in the present work.

\subsection{Legal sentence segmentation and structural analysis}

Accurate segmentation of legal documents into semantically meaningful units is a precondition for reliable information extraction. Bhattacharya et al.~[03\_05] present DeepRhole, a deep learning system for rhetorical role labeling of sentences in Indian and UK Supreme Court case documents, assigning functional labels — such as Fact, Argument, Precedent, and Final Judgement — to individual sentences. Their experimental evaluation demonstrates that Hierarchical BiLSTM architectures substantially outperform prior CRF-based methods that relied on handcrafted features, and that domain-specific pretraining using Law2Vec consistently improves performance. Crucially, they also report that some rhetorical label distinctions (e.g., Ratio of the Decision vs. Precedent) are inherently subjective, a finding that has direct methodological implications for any segmentation protocol that must reconcile automated predictions with human expert judgment.

Sheik et al.~[03\_07] address sentence boundary detection (SBD) in English legal texts, demonstrating that a hybrid CNN+CRF architecture outperforms both standalone deep learning models and open-source SBD libraries, achieving more than 4\% F1 improvement. Their out-of-domain evaluation on Indian legal text confirms the generalizability of context-aware delimiter classification to unseen legal corpora — a relevant consideration for the present work, where the Brazilian Portuguese legal register introduces additional boundary ambiguity through citation strings, procedural enumerations, and multi-clause sentence structures. Almalki et al.~[03\_03] propose a segmentation-and-aggregation framework for legal judgment prediction in Arabic, in which documents are segmented into sentence-level inputs, independently classified with AraBERT, and then aggregated to produce document-level decisions. Their success in overcoming the 512-token transformer limit through sentence-level decomposition provides a structural precedent for the LLM-assisted segmentation approach adopted in Stage3 of the present pipeline.

Ragazzi et al.~[03\_04] introduce LAWSUIT, a large-scale dataset for abstractive summarization of Italian constitutional court verdicts, and demonstrate that segmentation-based summarization pipelines outperform end-to-end sequence-to-sequence models across both full and few-shot settings. Their finding — that the uniform distribution of salient content throughout Italian legal verdicts prevents position-based shortcuts — resonates with the challenge encountered in Brazilian criminal labor-law decisions, where legally critical information (the conviction, the penalty, and the parties' identities) is similarly distributed across structurally heterogeneous document sections. Chen et al.~[03\_06] independently confirm the superiority of hierarchical segmentation in the context of long-document summarization using LLMs, showing that combining document decomposition with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting significantly reduces factual hallucination and improves coverage across scientific, governmental, and legal domains. Their ablation studies confirm that segmentation and reasoning guidance make independent, complementary contributions to output quality.

\subsection{Named entity recognition in legal and low-resource settings}

Legal NER is the most direct antecedent to Stage4 of the present pipeline. Wang et al.~[03\_02] propose AT-Flat, an adversarial training model that integrates task-shared word boundary signals from Chinese Word Segmentation (CWS) into a nested NER architecture for Chinese judicial documents. By combining character-word fusion via the Flat-Lattice Transformer with an adversarial multi-task framework, they address the nested entity structure that is also present in Brazilian legal text, where party names, judicial organizations, and statutory references frequently overlap. Their demonstration that adversarial boundary transfer improves NER performance in specialized legal corpora — evaluated on traffic accident and financial lending judgment datasets — validates the importance of domain-aware architectural design for legal NER.

Bellandi et al.~[01\_01] present an entity-centric infrastructure for Italian court judgments that combines machine learning and syntactic rule-based NLP services in an iterative annotation pipeline with a human-in-the-loop validation layer. Their co-design methodology, involving legal institutions and implementation partners alongside a scientific team, and their emphasis on building a sustainable, multi-purpose annotation infrastructure rather than a single-task system, closely mirrors the design philosophy of the present framework. The scalability challenges they identify — particularly the difficulty of maintaining annotation quality at national scale with limited expert involvement — are directly relevant to the Brazilian context, where the volume of labor-court decisions is substantial and expert annotation resources are scarce. Sakhaee and Wilson~[01\_02] demonstrate that information extraction approaches based on rule-based identification of citation expressions can achieve precision and recall exceeding 98\% when constructing legislation networks from large, noisy legal corpora. Their success in handling poor-quality scanned documents through approximate string matching underscores the practical value of preprocessing robustness — a principle instantiated in Stage1 of the present pipeline through aggressive PJe footer removal and document-type normalization.

Wang et al.~[03\_09] propose CLWKD, a cross-lingual semantic alignment framework that leverages multi-granularity mapping matrices and knowledge distillation to transfer domain-specific representations from high-resource languages to low-resource agglutinative languages. While developed for Mongolian-Chinese and Korean-Chinese NLP tasks, the framework's motivation — overcoming the joint challenges of domain-specific data scarcity and morphological complexity — is structurally analogous to the resource constraints faced in Brazilian Portuguese legal NLP, where both domain-adapted models and annotated legal corpora remain limited relative to English counterparts. The paper establishes cross-lingual knowledge distillation as a promising paradigm for extending legal NLP capabilities to underrepresented languages without requiring large-scale domain-specific annotated corpora.

Zhu et al.~[01\_03] design a knowledge graph retrieval system for Chinese public prosecutorial affairs, combining a bi-LSTM-CRF NER module with ontology-based graph storage and latent semantic indexing. Their multichannel input strategy — fusing text representations, word vectors, and entity vectors — and their integration of NER within a broader knowledge management infrastructure provide a relevant reference for the entity aggregation and output schema design in Stage4 of the present work. Similarly, Guha et al.~[03\_08] demonstrate that multi-modal document classification — combining VGG16 image features with Legal-BERTbase text representations — achieves F1-scores exceeding 97\% for document boundary detection in a Title Insurance context. Their confirmation that textual features are the primary determinant of classification performance, with visual features providing marginal but operationally significant gains, reinforces the text-centric design of the present pipeline.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
\subsection{Overview of the multi-layer pipeline}
Figure~\ref{fig:activity_pipeline} presents the end-to-end workflow adopted in this study. The process starts with document-type normalization and filtering (Stage1\_1), keeping only judicial sentences that are relevant to criminal-merit analysis. Next, Stage1\_2 applies task-adaptive preprocessing and generates two normalized outputs: one optimized for merit classification and one that preserves legal-text patterns for segmentation and NER. Stage2 performs penal-merit classification, Stage3 applies LLM-assisted segmentation to the conviction subset, and Stage4 performs legal-domain named-entity extraction over segmented content.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
\subsection{Merit classification performance}
Table~\ref{tab:encoder_comparison} compares three encoder backbones under the same downstream classifiers, using weighted F1 as the primary criterion. The best overall result is highlighted in bold and corresponds to BERTimbau-large with linear SVM ($F1=0.6152 \pm 0.0808$). Legal-BERT remains competitive and obtains the strongest XGBoost result ($F1=0.6114 \pm 0.0871$), while BERTimbau-base shows gains for Logistic Regression and Random Forest.

In practical terms, this indicates that domain adaptation in the encoder remains beneficial for the XGBoost configuration, while larger general-purpose Portuguese encoders can provide stronger performance with linear classifiers. Given this trade-off, both Legal-BERT+XGBoost and BERTimbau-large+SVM should be considered viable Stage2 operating points depending on interpretability and computational constraints.
