Abstract
Sentence boundary detection (SBD) represents an important first step in natural
language processing since accurately identifying sentence boundaries significantly
impacts downstream applications. Nevertheless, detecting sentence boundaries
within legal texts poses a unique and challenging problem due to their distinct structural and linguistic features. Our approach utilizes deep learning models to leverage
delimiter and surrounding context information as input, enabling precise detection
of sentence boundaries in English legal texts. We evaluate various deep learning
models, including domain-specific transformer models like LegalBERT and CaseLawBERT. To assess the efficacy of our deep learning models, we compare them with
a state-of-the-art domain-specific statistical conditional random field (CRF) model.
After considering model size, F1-score, and inference time, we identify the Convolutional Neural Network Model (CNN) as the top-performing deep learning model. To
further enhance performance, we integrate the features of the CNN model into the
subsequent CRF model, creating a hybrid architecture that combines the strengths
of both models. Our experiments demonstrate that the hybrid model outperforms
the baseline model, achieving a 4% improvement in the F1-score. Additional experiments showcase the superiority of the hybrid model over SBD open-source libraries when confronted with an out-of-domain test set. These findings underscore the
importance of efficient SBD in legal texts and emphasize the advantages of employing deep learning models and hybrid architectures to achieve optimal performance.
Keywords Natural language processing · Sentence boundary detection · Deep
learning · Transformer · LegalBERT · CaseLawBERT · CNN · CRF

1 Introduction
Sentence Boundary Detection (SBD) is an essential component of Natural Language Processing (NLP), which involves identifying the boundaries between sentences in a given text. The proper delineation of sentence boundaries is crucial in
any NLP application, as incorrect boundaries can greatly distort the NLP pipeline
for many downstream applications (Savelka et al. 2017). While humans can easily
identify and separate sentences within a given text, developing automatic sentence boundary detection algorithms is a complex task in NLP (Păiş and Tufiş
2021). The task has been extensively studied in general domains (Read et al.
2012), but detecting sentence boundaries in specialized corpora such as legal
text poses significant challenges. The legal text is characterized by lengthy and
intricate sentences with lists, citations, abbreviations, and law-specific keywords,
often making it difficult to compose actual sentences from legal documents
(Savelka et al. 2017). In addition, legal documents of the same kind, like laws or
court decisions, can vary significantly in structure and content (Allen and Lysaght
2015), making it difficult to separate one sentence from another. This is where
a sentence split system comes in, which must solve these problems by identifying potential boundary spots and accurately pinpointing sentence-ending markers.
Therefore, developing accurate SBD algorithms for legal text is a critical research
area in NLP and useful in many applications like legal argument mining (Habernal et al. 2023; Chen et al. 2022), legal text summarization (Jain et al. 2023;
Deroy et al. 2023), rhetorical role segmentation (Saravanan and Ravindran 2010;
Bhattacharya et al. 2021), legal information retrieval (Chalkidis and Androutsopoulos 2017; Mandal et al. 2021) etc.
One of the primary concerns with SBD for legal documents is the ambiguous
nature of delimiters involved in it Allen and Lysaght (2015). For example, the
period delimiter may indicate the boundary of a line, an abbreviation, a numerical
value, or part of a citation in legal text (Sheik et al. 2022). To achieve this goal, a
sophisticated algorithm is needed to identify and categorize the diverse range of
punctuation marks employed in legal documents while considering their contextual usage. The motivation for this work is to use a deep learning framework that
utilizes the contextual information surrounding delimiters for SBD, treating it as
a sequence classification problem in English legal text. Furthermore, a performance improvement is demonstrated using a hybrid architecture incorporating the
state-of-the-art statistical Conditional Random Field (CRF) model and the Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The performance of this hybrid model is also
tested in the Indian domain and compared with the standard open-source libraries

2 Related work
The SBD has been extensively studied in the NLP literature, and several algorithms
and models have been proposed to identify sentence boundaries in a generic corpus (Read et al. 2012). However, these algorithms and models face challenges when
dealing with a specialized domain. To address this, our literature review is organized into three sections. Firstly, we examine the literature on SBD in general. Next,
we explore works focused on specialized scientific, medical, and finance domains.
Lastly, we present existing research pertaining to the legal domain, which possesses
distinct language, structure, and content.
2.1 Generic sentence boundary detection
In the subsequent sub-sections, we categorize the existing literature into rule-based,
supervised, and unsupervised approaches, providing a comprehensive classification
of the SBD methods.
2.1.1 Rule‑based models
Sentence boundary detection is performed by various systems using hand-crafted
rules and regular expressions. These approaches are favored because the errors they
produce can be easily understood, and the rules can be adjusted gradually. One of

522

R. Sheik et al.

the earliest rule-based systems, described by Grefenstette and Tapanainen (1994),
utilized a set of regular expressions to differentiate periods used in abbreviations,
numbers, email addresses, and web addresses from those indicating the end of a sentence. However, this system solely focused on periods as sentence boundaries and
achieved an accuracy of 93.78%. Later (Mikheev 2000) treats sentence segmentation
with rules to determine whether the words adjacent to a potential sentence boundary
are abbreviations or proper names. They adopted a POS tagging approach to sentence splitting, producing highly portable models across different corpora. A more
recent rule-based approach—PySBD (Pragmatic Sentence Boundary Disambiguation) (Sadvilkar and Neumann 2020) utilizes a combination of exceptions and regular expression rules. It has proven to be a robust, open-source solution for sentence
boundary detection in 22 languages. However, rule-based approaches present challenges when scaling to multiple languages since they require extensive manual effort
for each language. Consequently, many studies in sentence boundary detection have
moved away from rule-based techniques due to their lack of sustainability and efficiency to the unseen corpus.
2.1.2 Supervised models
In early SBD research, an approach proposed by Riley (1989) utilized decision trees
to analyze the contextual lexical features surrounding periods, determining whether
they indicate sentence boundaries. This method incorporated statistical features,
including word probabilities derived from a 25 million-word corpus, achieving
an impressive accuracy of 99.8% on the widely recognized Brown Corpus (Francis and Kucera 1979). Subsequently, the SATZ architecture introduced by Palmer
and Hearst (1997) employed either a decision tree or a neural network, leveraging
POS features. The SATZ architecture achieved an accuracy of 98.5% when evaluated on the Wall Street Journal (WSJ) dataset. Another statistical system, Reynar
and Ratnaparkhi (1997), utilized a maximum entropy classifier that considered simpler lexical features of the words preceding and following potential delimiters and
attained accuracy scores of 98.8% and 97.9% on both the WSJ text and the Brown
Corpus, respectively. In the study, Wong and Chao (2010) introduced an incremental algorithm that employed various features, including capitalization, word length,
potential punctuation, and the presence of specific signs like ‘$’ and numbers. These
features were extracted from trigram contexts within a training corpus to detect sentence boundaries. Remarkably, their approach obtained 99.98% accuracy when performed on the Brown corpus.
Following a similar approach, Splitta (Gillick 2009) further improved the method
by employing Support Vector Machines (SVM) with lexical features. Splitta concentrated on the specific task of identifying sentence boundaries using periods and
demonstrated enhanced performance. However, the aforementioned approaches
restricted sentence boundaries to punctuation marks, excluding non-punctuation
characters from consideration. The SpaCy library (Honnibal et al. 2020) addressed
this limitation by jointly learning dependency parsing and sentence segmentation
using a transition-based parser on annotated data. SpaCy did not treat punctuation
marks as special entities, allowing for more flexible sentence boundary detection.

2.1.3 Unsupervised and self‑supervised models
Unsupervised methods aim to acquire segmentation knowledge solely from the
raw, unsegmented text. Punkt (Kiss and Strunk 2006) is a completely unsupervised
system that recognizes abbreviations, initials, and ordinal numbers. It introduces a
language-agnostic, unsupervised technique and emphasizes that abbreviations significantly cause ambiguity in detecting sentence boundaries. To construct a highly
accurate abbreviation detector, Punkt utilizes collocation detection (Dunning 1994).
In recent advancements, Ersatz (Wicks and Post 2021) introduced a self-supervised
model by modernizing the approach proposed by Riley (1989) incorporating a transformer architecture (Vaswani et al. 2017) that considered sub-words as contextual
information around punctuation marks. This facilitates training without labeled data,
as line breaks inherently divide the text into paragraphs. In contrast to this method,
a recently developed WtP model (Minixhofer et al. 2023) empowers self-supervised
sentence segmentation without depending on punctuation or making assumptions
specific to a particular language. The versatility of this approach was demonstrated
in five different sizes, covering a wide range of 85 languages.
However, in specialized domains, the task of SBD is notably influenced by
domain-specific data and characteristics. This is primarily because such domains
frequently demonstrate deviations from conventional sentence structures, variability
in sentence lengths, and the utilization of non-standard punctuation. The authors in
Read et al. (2012) note a decline in performance when transitioning from general
corpora to domain-specific corpora. Their findings indicate that incorporating a reasonable level of analysis of document structure contributes to increased overall SBD
performance.

2.2 Domain‑specific sentence boundary detection
The initial system developed by Reynar and Ratnaparkhi (1997) focused on achieving superior performance within a specific domain. This model utilized features
specifically designed for the English financial language, such as identifying honorifics and corporate designations. Rather than treating sentence segmentation as
a standalone task, the approach framed it as a disambiguation task involving targeted delimiters. Previous work limited evaluation on datasets such as the Brown
corpus and WSJ and discussed issues with SBD while assuming characteristics of
high-quality text, such as appropriate formatting, grammatically correct sentences,
and the absence of spelling errors. The work (Griffis et al. 2016) evaluated widely
used NLP toolkits to assess their performance on SBD tasks using clinical domain
corpora. The study by López and Pardo (2015) primarily focused on SBD in informal content generated by users found across various online platforms. The work by
Rudrapal et al. (2015) specifically conducted research on SBD within the context
of social media. Moreover, there has been considerable interest in SBD for speech
transcriptions, as automated recognition systems often rely on identifying sentence
segments within these transcripts. Recent studies (Wang et al. 2019; Rehbein et al.

2020) have investigated the challenges of SBD in speech, which lacks both punctuation and the structured nature of the written text.
2.3 Legal sentence boundary detection
The task of sentence boundary detection specific to the legal domain commenced
with creating a dataset for US Adjudicatory decisions (Savelka et al. 2017) and
explored the use of CRF models (Lafferty et al. 2001). An aggressive tokenization technique was employed, and basic textual features such as token length, digits, spaces, upper/lower case, etc., were considered. These features were utilized
to develop multiple CRF models, which resulted in increased inference time. In a
related investigation (Sanchez 2019), the identical dataset was investigated using
Punkt, CRF, and Bidirectional LSTM neural network architecture. Enhancements
were made to the Punkt model by training it with an updated set of abbreviations
tailored for the legal text-domain. The neural network architecture employed word2vec embeddings and a stacked BiLSTM network with a softmax layer for output.
However, this classification architecture did not outperform the performance of the
CRF model. The following research (Sheik et al. 2022) has explored the application
of deep learning models for SBD in the legal domain. Inspired by previous work
(Schweter and Ahmed 2019), finding sentence boundaries is treated as a classification problem, where models are trained to identify potential sentence-ending period
markers based on the surrounding context. A fixed-size context window consists of
concatenating the left and right contexts of the period symbol as input to the model.
The CNN model outperformed other deep learning models and the pySBD model.
In contrast, the trained CNN model showcased satisfactory performance without
necessitating extensive feature engineering while also exhibiting significantly faster
inference times compared to state-of-the-art CRF models (Sanchez 2019). However,
a primary limitation of the CNN model lies in its sole reliance on the period symbol
as a delimiter, necessitating further exploration into other punctuation marks that
indicate sentence boundaries.
Our research aims to further enhance the context-specific architecture by incorporating additional delimiters into the model. To achieve this, we introduce the context and the delimiter as input to the models, allowing for a more comprehensive
understanding of the sentence segmentation task. In addition to processing English text, Glaser et al. (2021) curated a German dataset, while an even more recent
development by Brugger et al. (2023) introduced a diverse legal dataset specifically
designed for multilingual models, encompassing six distinct languages. The literature in the legal domain pertaining to models, datasets, and other findings is summarized in Table 1.

yet explored the utilization of hybrid models that combine the deep learning CNN
framework with the statistical CRF model. Previous SBD approaches in the legal

and other feature engineering tasks. Our proposed neural network models tackle the

3.1 Legal sentence structure
In legal documents, sentences generally adhere to a standard structure, encompassing a
subject, object, and verb in the correct grammatical order. Punctuation marks like periods, semicolons, question marks, or colons typically terminate these sentences (Păiş
and Tufiş 2021). Legal language is characterized by many long compounds and complex sentences, often comprising multiple clauses contributing to their extensive length.
There have been documented instances of sentences in a legal language containing as
many as 494 words, underscoring the long and complex nature of this linguistic style
(Lavery 1921). Complexity in legal sentences arises from several factors, including
excessive modifications, over-caution, the use of embedded clauses, atypical word order,
and the inclusion of words or phrases in unconventional contexts that deviate from typical discourse patterns.
Headlines play a prominent role in legal documents, establishing the text’s structure and highlighting relationships between different parts. It’s important to note that
certain headlines may incorporate case names, adding complexity to their interpretation. Citations, commonly found in judgments, serve to connect various cases, statutes, or laws. Lists are a noticeable structural component in legal documents that often
pose challenges in determining their sentence boundaries. For instance, in enumerated
lists, each item of the enumeration is treated as an individual sentence to ensure consistency within the list (Savelka et al. 2017). These lists can be embedded within sentences featuring a variable middle part, where different subjects or objects are possible
for a clause. Data fields containing information such as dates, people, locations, and
case names require special attention during analysis. Endnotes/footnotes should also be
addressed effectively. Legal documents can exhibit mixed cases involving the aforementioned structures. Moreover, even within the same legal document type, there exists
significant variation (Allen and Lysaght 2015), including instances of parentheses
within sentences. Figure 1 illustrates a few examples of intricate and lengthy sentence
structure regarding the use of the English language in the Indian legal domain.

We constructed five distinct neural network architectures: Long Short Term
Memory (LSTM) (Gers et al. 2000), Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) (Chung et al.
2014), Bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM), Bidirectional GRU (BiGRU), and Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). Additionally, the BiLSTM and BiGRU models were
trained with attention mechanisms (Bahdanau et al. 2015), enabling them to focus
on key segments within the input. The following subsection briefly describes the
deep learning models used in the implementation.
4.1.1 LSTM
For our experiments, we utilize a standard LSTM network with an embedding
dimension of 128 and a hidden dimension of 256. To introduce some regularization,

530

R. Sheik et al.

we apply a dropout probability of 0.2 to the hidden layer. The resulting output is
then fed into a dense layer with a sigmoid activation function for classification.
4.1.2 BiLSTM
To enhance the contextual understanding, we employ a Bidirectional LSTM structure that handles input in both the forward and backward directions. This results
in an input size of 512 when passed to the following dense layer. The remaining
parameters and configurations remain comparable to the LSTM design. Later, we
introduce attention weights to integrate an attention technique to the BiLSTM architecture (Lin et al. 2017). Including attention determines the soft alignment scores
between each hidden state and the final hidden state of the LSTM. This enables the
model to capture the comprehensive interconnections between the input elements
and the output.
4.1.3 GRU​
The GRU architecture employs 256 hidden states and an embedding size of 128. A
dropout probability of 0.2 is applied after the hidden layer during training. GRU is a
more compact variant of LSTM, as it does not feature an explicit memory unit.
4.1.4 BiGRU​
Similar to the BiLSTM, the BiGRU architecture shares the same design but utilizes
GRU instead of LSTM. By incorporating attention into the BiGRU architecture, the
model allows for interactions between the inputs to determine which parts deserve
heightened attention. These dynamic interactions, in conjunction with attention
scores, are subsequently synthesized to generate the final output representations.
4.1.5 CNN
Figure 2 shows our CNN architecture, which employs a 1D convolutional layer
encompassing six filters with a kernel size of five. The resulting output from the
convolutional filter is concatenated to construct a comprehensive feature vector,
subsequently propagated through a global max pooling layer. Prior to the prediction
layer, a 250-dimensional hidden layer employing ReLU activation (Agarap 2018) is
employed. During training, the model employs a learning rate of 0.001 and incorporates dropout with a probability of 0.2.

4.2.1 LegalBERT
LEGAL-BERT is a suite of BERT models specifically developed to facilitate legal
NLP research. The work (Chalkidis et al. 2020) explores three distinct options of
LegalBERT for domain adaptation: (i) utilizing the original BERT model without
any modifications, (ii) augmenting pre-training with additional domain-specific corpus, and (iii) training BERT from scratch using domain-specific data. These models
were trained on datasets from EU laws, ECHR cases, and US contracts. Our experiments focus on the “legal-bert-base-uncased" variant, a model trained from scratch
on legal texts.
4.2.2 CaseLawBERT
CaseLawBERT (Zheng et al. 2021) is a model trained on Harvard Law case corpus2 encompassing 3,446,187 legal decisions across various federal and state courts.
For the training objective, the model begins with the base BERT model, bert-baseuncased (uncased, 110 M parameters), and undergoes further training for an additional 1 M steps. This training is performed on the Masked Language Model (MLM)
and Next Sentence Prediction (NSP) objectives.

4.4 Hybrid model
In our proposed hybrid architecture, we treated SBD as a sequence labeling problem
and used features from the deep learning-based CNN to train the statistical CRF
model. CRF model is a custom SBD system, wherein we trained several conditional
random field models based on simple low-level features. More complex features can
help to improve the performance even further, as is evident from the literature that
the inclusion of special tokens is important for good performance in Law documents
(Glaser et al. 2021). The CNN model is adept at capturing intricate patterns and
features from the input data, making it particularly effective for tasks such as feature
extraction in NLP (Chollampatt and Ng 2018). In our hybrid setup, the CNN generates valuable insights from the character-level tokenization of legal texts. These
extracted features, especially the indication of delimiters, were then integrated into
the CRF model. The CRF model, on the other hand, excels in utilizing sequential
dependencies and contextual information. By combining the strengths of both models, we can achieve a synergistic effect and increase the performance of our SBD
system. Figure 3 presents the overall architecture of the hybrid model. The data processing layer was used to extract essential input for the CNN model and to tokenize
the text for the subsequent feature assignment process. The feature extraction layer
specifically focused on extracting and incorporating the output feature corresponding to the isdelimiter condition from the CNN model. This feature is combined with
other relevant features within the CRF model to optimize the effectiveness of feature
assignment. Finally, the CRF layer used the assigned features associated with each
token to predict the appropriate labels for the observation sequence.
Algorithm 2 illustrates the steps involved in the hybrid model. The CNN-CRF
hybrid model predicts label sequences for input text sequences. It tokenizes the input
text and extracts the delimiter D and its contextual features using the CNN Input
Extractor. An embedding layer obtains the embedded representation ED of D. The

6.3 Discussion
Deep learning models have transformed various NLP applications, showing significant differences in performance. The study by Collobert et al. (2011) demonstrated
that a simple deep learning framework outperforms most state-of-the-art approaches
in several NLP tasks. For our task, which involves sequence classification, we
sought to find the best deep learning model, starting from Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and progressing to more powerful transformer models. RNNs were
the initial choice due to their sequential processing capabilities, but studies (Chung
et al. 2014) demonstrated the superiority of gated units like LSTM and GRU over
traditional RNNs. We modified the basic framework of these gated RNNs by adding
bidirectional and attention techniques. Additionally, we explored the use of CNN
for sentence modeling. As RNNs aim to build a composition of arbitrarily long
sentences, the CNNs focus on extracting essential n-gram features from input sentences, generating valuable latent semantic representations that prove advantageous
for downstream tasks (Young et al. 2018).
The results presented in Table 7 highlight the superior performance of the CNN
model over other neural network architectures, as well as the CRF model. Remarkably, when compared to other deep learning models, Table 5 demonstrates that CNN
models exhibit shorter training times, while transformer models require nearly 40
times longer training. Also, domain-specific transformer models require more training data in order to achieve substantial results. LEGAL-BERT and CaselawBERT
models are challenging to implement due to their complex attention mechanism and
large model size. Moreover, due to the lack of substantial training data and taking
into account the use of input embeddings at the sub-word level, we could see a loss
in performance. Furthermore, it is important to highlight that the CNN model exhibits the fastest inference time (see Table 8), surpassing even the CRF models, mainly
due to the extensive feature engineering inherent in the CRF model architecture.
This efficiency in training and inference times positions the CNN model as the best
model with less computational cost for the proposed hybrid architecture. These findings provide strong support for the rationale behind incorporating the CNN model
into our hybrid framework.
We employed the McNemer Statistical test (McNemar 1947) to compare the performance of the top two classifiers, the statistical CRF and deep learning CNN. The
test is based on a 2 × 2 contingency table that shows the frequency distribution of the
outcomes of the two models’ predictions on the same dataset. The null hypothesis for
McNemar’s test assumes that the two models have the same performance and contribute to the same type of errors. The alternative hypothesis is that there is a difference in
their performance. McNemar’s test calculates a chi-square statistic that follows a chisquare distribution with one degree of freedom (Dietterich 1998). The p-value associated with the chi-square statistic is then compared to a chosen significance level (e.g.,
0.05) to determine the statistical significance of the observed differences. The obtained
p-value is way below this significance level, which suggests that there is a significant
difference in performance between the two models, rejecting the null hypothesis.

7 Conclusions and future work
In this paper, we have presented a deep learning approach to address the challenging problem of detecting sentence boundaries in legal text. Our approach leverages the context surrounding the delimiter for detecting sentence boundaries in
the legal text as a sequence classification problem. We evaluated the performance
of various deep learning models and identified the best-performing CNN model
based on model size, F1 score, and inference time. We compared the performance
of our models with the state-of-art CRF model. Our evaluation results demonstrate the superiority of our proposed deep learning approach over the baselines,
with significant improvements in the F1 score. We also identified the complementary errors generated by the top-performing models to provide valuable insights
into the limitations of current SBD approaches. To further improve the performance of our approach, we incorporated the features of the best-performing CNN
model into a statistical CRF model to obtain a hybrid architecture that leverages
the strengths of both models. Our hybrid model achieved an F1 score improvement of over 4% compared to the individual models, demonstrating the effectiveness of combining deep learning models with traditional statistical models. Also,
our hybrid model exhibits its effectiveness and robustness in accurately detecting sentence boundaries in the context of Indian legal text compared to standard open-source libraries. This achievement highlights the model’s capability
to handle the unique linguistic and structural features inherent to Indian legal
documents.
Our paper needs an assessment of how these tools impact downstream tasks,
presenting an opportunity for future investigation. A subtle enhancement in this
prepossessing step can significantly impact the performance of subsequent NLP
applications. Conducting such an evaluation would be an evident direction for
further research. While it is conceivable that minor disparities among the topperforming models may not significantly influence specific tasks, this study illuminates the nature of these variations, offering valuable insights. Another possible next step involves the integration of rule-based approaches into the existing
architecture, specifically designed to address the drawn-out error samples. Additionally, exploring the incorporation of ensemble techniques presents a promising opportunity to enhance the SBD process further. Moreover, the current corpus of legal texts needs more representation in other areas like tax documents,
dockets, and headnotes as well as the absence of multilingual datasets for training
and evaluation. Indeed, the deep learning model employed in our research holds
potential for cross-linguistic capabilities, which can be trained on any language
because it works on character level and can scale to other languages. While the
features in CRF are particularly tailored to the English Language, many of the
features are also applicable to other languages, such as (isdigit, length, etc.,) and
add custom features of the particular language into the proposed model, making
it a valuable approach to extend it to other languages. The segmentation of sentences using our deep learning models is finely tuned at the character level, eliminating the necessity for language-specific tokenization processes. This flexibility
