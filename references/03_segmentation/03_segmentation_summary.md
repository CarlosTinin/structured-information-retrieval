## 03_01 — The use of information technology for international transfer pricing in multinational enterprises

**Authors:** Lars Hemling, Jacob Christian Plesner Rossing, Andreas Hoffjan  
**Journal:** International Journal of Accounting Information Systems (2022), vol. 44, 100546  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.accinf.2021.100546

> **Note:** This article is thematically outside the NLP/legal-AI domain. It concerns ERP systems and tax compliance in multinational enterprises and was included as part of the retrieved reference set.

Hemling, Rossing, and Hoffjan investigate the extent to which multinational enterprises (MNEs) leverage information technology (IT) to manage international transfer pricing (ITP) — the practice of setting prices for transactions between affiliated entities within a corporate group across different tax jurisdictions. The study is motivated by the heightened regulatory environment following the OECD's Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) action plan, which has intensified compliance documentation requirements for MNEs and increased scrutiny from tax authorities worldwide. Given this context, the authors ask to what degree MNEs have automated their ITP processes and what systemic and organizational factors explain the observed level of automation.

The empirical investigation draws on 21 semi-structured interviews conducted with in-house accounting and tax professionals employed by MNEs, primarily headquartered in Germany. The findings reveal that the actual adoption of IT for ITP management is surprisingly limited. While some degree of automation is observed in the workflow management of transfer pricing documentation — particularly following the 2017 revision of the OECD Guidelines — the core processes of transfer price setting and intercompany transaction processing remain largely manual, often relying on Excel-based spreadsheet models developed in-house. This dependency on manual processes is identified as a source of data integrity risk and a potential liability when tax authorities demand traceable, auditable data flows.

The authors attribute the limited automation to two interconnected categories of barriers. At the system level, fragmented enterprise resource planning (ERP) environments — where multiple legacy systems coexist without full integration — prevent the seamless extraction of legal-entity-level data required for ITP compliance. At the individual level, management accountants and IT specialists, who dominate ERP design decisions, prioritize divisional management reporting needs over the legal-entity data segmentation required by tax departments. This functional hierarchy effectively marginalizes the tax department's influence over ERP configuration, perpetuating dependence on manual workarounds.

The theoretical contribution of the paper draws on contingency theory and institutional theory (specifically the taxonomy of mimetic, coercive, and normative isomorphic pressures) to interpret the observed patterns of ITP automation. The authors find that the coercive pressure exerted by OECD-based tax regulations is the primary driver of the limited automation that does occur, particularly in the documentation domain. However, this pressure has not yet been sufficient to overcome the structural and organizational inertia that constrains broader ITP automation, suggesting that more direct regulatory mandates or significant shifts in ERP design philosophy may be necessary to drive meaningful change.

The paper concludes with practical implications for CFOs, tax directors, and ERP vendors, arguing that standardized ITP policies and greater technological affinity within tax departments are prerequisites for effective automation. The study's main limitations include its geographic focus on German-headquartered MNEs and the exclusion of perspectives from external tax consultants and ITP software developers. Future research is encouraged to examine how specific ERP platforms and add-on ITP software solutions can better accommodate tax compliance objectives, and to investigate how different organizational configurations and value chain structures affect the feasibility of ITP automation.

## 03_02 — Adversarial training flat-lattice transformer for named entity recognition of Chinese legal texts

**Authors:** Jiabao Wang, Kaixuan Wang, Yang Weng, Xin Li  
**Journal:** Artificial Intelligence and Law (2025)  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10506-025-09476-4

Wang et al. address the problem of named entity recognition (NER) in Chinese judicial judgment documents, a task characterized by two compounding challenges: the structural complexity of Chinese text — which lacks word boundaries and exhibits high lexical ambiguity — and the domain-specific nature of legal entities, which are frequently nested and defined by specialized professional terminology. With nearly 100 million judgment documents publicly available in China's online judicial systems, the automated extraction of entities such as defendants, judicial organizations, legal articles, and case-specific facts has significant potential to reduce the manual workload of legal professionals and improve the efficiency of court operations. However, existing NER methods, even those achieving strong performance in general Chinese NLP tasks, fail to adequately handle the nested structure and blurred entity boundaries characteristic of judicial text.

To address these limitations, the authors propose AT-Flat, a novel NER model that integrates adversarial training with the Flat-Lattice Transformer (FLAT) architecture. FLAT encodes both character-level and word-level information within a unified sequence representation, allowing the model to exploit lexical knowledge without the information loss associated with traditional word segmentation preprocessing. This character-word fusion is complemented by a Conditional Random Field (CRF) decoder, which enforces globally consistent label sequences and prevents the generation of invalid entity boundary combinations. The adversarial component of AT-Flat introduces a dual-task training framework in which the model jointly learns Chinese Word Segmentation (CWS) and NER, extracting shared word boundary features across both tasks while filtering out CWS-specific noise that could degrade NER performance.

The adversarial training mechanism is identified as the key novelty of the work and the first application of adversarial multi-task learning to nested NER in Chinese judicial documents. By treating CWS and NER as related but distinct sequence labeling problems, the framework enables the model to leverage the rich word boundary signals available from CWS supervision without being constrained by the segmentation errors that CWS systems inevitably introduce in specialized legal vocabularies. This design is particularly well-suited to the judicial domain, where professional terms such as institutional names and legal citations often fall outside the coverage of general-purpose CWS lexicons.

Experimental evaluation is conducted on two proprietary Chinese judgment document datasets — covering traffic accident and financial lending cases — as well as three public Chinese NER benchmarks. The results demonstrate that AT-Flat consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across all datasets, confirming the effectiveness of combining character-word fusion with adversarial boundary transfer. The performance advantage is most pronounced on the judicial datasets, where the density of nested and domain-specific entities is highest, validating the domain applicability of the proposed approach.

The authors acknowledge two principal limitations of AT-Flat: its constrained capacity to model long-range dependencies within lengthy legal documents, and potential degradation in inference efficiency as input length increases — both of which may complicate deployment in production legal environments. Future work is planned to extend the model's applicability to other legal subdomains and longer document types. The paper makes a substantive contribution to the nascent field of Chinese legal NLP by demonstrating that adversarial multi-task learning, combined with lattice-based character-word representations, provides an effective and generalizable approach to the nested NER problem in specialized legal corpora.

## 03_03 — Legal Judgment Prediction in the Saudi Arabian Commercial Court

**Authors:** Ashwaq Almalki, Safa Alsafari, Noura M. Alotaibi  
**Journal:** Future Internet (2025), vol. 17, 439  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3390/fi17100439

Almalki, Alsafari, and Alotaibi investigate the automated prediction of legal judgment outcomes in Saudi Arabian commercial court cases, contributing to the growing field of Legal Judgment Prediction (LJP) for Arabic-language legal systems. The work is motivated by the significant increase in the volume of cases filed with Saudi courts — a 23.56% growth between 2021 and 2024 — and the potential of AI-assisted decision support to reduce case processing times and judicial workloads. The authors identify a critical gap in the LJP literature: while Chinese and English legal systems have been extensively studied, Arabic-language LJP systems remain scarce, and those that do exist typically rely on small datasets or focus on narrow legal subdomains such as personal-status cases, limiting their generalizability to commercial law contexts.

The core methodological challenge addressed in this paper is the input length limitation of transformer-based models, which are typically constrained to 512 tokens — far shorter than the average commercial court ruling. To overcome this, the authors propose a two-step segmentation-and-aggregation framework. In the first step, each document's Fact section is segmented into individual sentences, each of which is independently classified using AraBERT v2, a pretrained Arabic BERT model. In the second step, the resulting sentence-level predictions are aggregated into a document-level decision using four strategies: Mean, Max, Confidence-Weighted, and Positional aggregation. This architecture enables the processing of full-length documents without truncation while remaining compatible with standard transformer infrastructure.

The framework is evaluated on a dataset of 19,822 cases from the Saudi Commercial Court — the largest Arabic LJP dataset reported in the literature to date — with binary outcome labels (Accepted vs. Rejected). The Confidence-Weighted aggregation method, which assigns higher weight to sentences for which the classifier expresses high certainty, achieves the best overall performance, with an accuracy of 85.62% and an F1-score of 85.61%. Error analysis reveals that the system exhibits higher precision for Rejected cases than for Accepted ones, and that prediction accuracy decreases for longer documents and for certain case types, indicating that document length and domain-specific legal complexity remain challenges for the current architecture.

The segmentation-and-aggregation paradigm employed in this work has broader implications for legal NLP beyond the specific Arabic context. By decomposing document-level classification into sentence-level predictions that are subsequently integrated, the framework effectively transforms a long-document problem into a more tractable short-text problem, enabling the direct application of pretrained transformer models without architectural modifications. The interpretability benefit of sentence-level predictions is also noted: legal professionals can inspect which individual sentences most influence the document-level outcome, providing a form of local explanation that supports human oversight of the system.

The authors are careful to frame the proposed system as a decision-support tool rather than a replacement for judicial discretion, emphasizing that final legal determinations must remain the responsibility of human judges. Future research directions include domain-adaptive pretraining on large-scale Arabic legal corpora, the development of architectures capable of capturing long-range contextual dependencies across sentences, and the extension of the prediction task to more nuanced outcome categories, including cases with mixed or partial judgments. The paper represents a significant methodological and empirical contribution to Arabic legal NLP and to the broader challenge of applying transformer-based models to long, specialized legal documents.

## 03_04 — LAWSUIT: a LArge expert-Written SUmmarization dataset of ITalian constitutional court verdicts

**Authors:** Luca Ragazzi, Gianluca Moro, Stefano Guidi, Giacomo Frisoni  
**Journal:** Artificial Intelligence and Law (2025), vol. 33, pp. 1151–1187  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10506-024-09414-w

Ragazzi et al. present LAWSUIT, the first large-scale dataset specifically constructed for abstractive summarization of Italian legal verdicts, addressing a well-recognized deficiency in non-English legal NLP resources. The dataset comprises 14,000 verdicts issued by the Constitutional Court of the Italian Republic (CCIR), each paired with an expert-authored abstractive maxim — a concise, legally authoritative synopsis crafted by highly qualified legal experts. The construction of LAWSUIT responds to the dual challenge of document complexity in the legal domain — where texts routinely span thousands of words filled with specialized jargon — and the near-exclusive focus of existing legal summarization benchmarks on English-language sources, which limits research applicability for Italian legal practitioners and scholars.

LAWSUIT presents a particularly demanding summarization task due to three characteristic features of its source documents. First, the source texts and target summaries are substantially longer than those in existing Italian summarization datasets (+269% and +589%, respectively), requiring models capable of processing and representing very long inputs. Second, and most distinctively, the salient content in LAWSUIT source documents is distributed more uniformly throughout the text rather than concentrated in specific sections such as introductions or conclusions. This characteristic contrasts with many existing legal summarization benchmarks and prevents models from relying on positional heuristics — a common shortcut in extractive and abstractive systems alike. Third, unlike many large-scale summarization datasets constructed through automatic pairing of documents with existing metadata, both inputs and targets in LAWSUIT are authored by domain experts, ensuring high quality and legal accuracy.

The paper presents an extensive experimental evaluation comparing sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) models with segmentation-based summarization approaches. The segmentation-based pipeline — which divides source documents into coherent text chunks before summarizing each segment and composing the final output — consistently outperforms direct seq2seq methods in both full-data and few-shot settings. This finding is theoretically important: it suggests that hierarchical decomposition of the input is not merely a practical workaround for context-length limitations but represents a substantively better inductive strategy for handling the uniform distribution of salient content in long legal documents.

The release of LAWSUIT as an open dataset is positioned as the paper's most significant practical contribution. By making 14,000 expert-annotated verdict-summary pairs publicly available, the authors provide a shared benchmark that can support the development and comparison of legal summarization systems, stimulate research into long-document abstractive summarization beyond the English language, and facilitate the training of models tailored to Italian constitutional law. The dataset's design also makes it suitable for related tasks, including legal text classification and legal reasoning, which the authors identify as directions for future extensions.

The paper concludes by acknowledging important limitations: the absence of Italian-language comparison baselines requires cross-linguistic comparisons with English datasets, introducing potential biases; and the segmentation-based approach used as the top-performing baseline requires the generation of at least one sentence per chunk, which may limit scalability to settings requiring very concise summaries. Future work is expected to explore graph-based representations of legal documents to improve segmentation efficiency and to extend the dataset to new legal reasoning and classification tasks, broadening the impact of LAWSUIT beyond the summarization domain.

## 03_05 — DeepRhole: deep learning for rhetorical role labeling of sentences in legal case documents

**Authors:** Paheli Bhattacharya et al.  
**Journal:** Artificial Intelligence and Law (2023), vol. 31, pp. 53–90  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10506-021-09304-5

Bhattacharya et al. present DeepRhole, a comprehensive study on the automatic identification of rhetorical roles in sentences of legal case documents, constituting one of the most rigorous and broad-scoped treatments of this problem in the legal NLP literature. Rhetorical role labeling — the assignment of semantic function labels such as Fact, Argument, Precedent, Statute, and Final Judgement to individual sentences — is identified as a foundational task in legal analytics that enables a range of downstream applications including semantic search, automatic summarization, case similarity computation, and judicial decision support. The central challenge of the task lies in the structural variability of legal documents, which differ substantially across jurisdictions and legal domains, and in the inherent subjectivity of certain rhetorical distinctions, which leads to disagreement even among trained legal professionals.

A significant portion of the paper is devoted to the construction of two annotated datasets covering documents from the Supreme Courts of India and the United Kingdom, and to a systematic inter-annotator agreement (IAA) analysis conducted among senior law students. The IAA study reveals that while overall agreement is acceptable, specific pairs of labels — most notably Ratio of the Decision and Precedents — are inherently difficult to distinguish, with disagreement arising from genuine semantic overlap rather than annotator inconsistency. This finding has important implications for the design of evaluation metrics and for the interpretation of model performance: labels with high inter-annotator subjectivity should not be expected to achieve the same classification accuracy as labels with clear semantic boundaries, regardless of model sophistication.

The paper evaluates four families of neural models for the classification task: Hierarchical BiLSTM (Hier-BiLSTM), Hier-BiLSTM combined with a CRF decoder, attention-augmented variants of both, and Transformer-based models incorporating BERT and LegalBERT. All models operate at the sentence level while encoding inter-sentence dependencies through hierarchical or sequential architectures, which is shown to be critical for performance given that rhetorical role transitions follow sequential patterns in legal documents. The best-performing model achieves a macro F1-score of 0.821 on the Indian dataset and 0.600 on the UK dataset, with the substantial gap between jurisdictions attributed to differences in document structure and the relative size of the respective training sets.

Several important empirical findings emerge from the experimental analysis. Domain-specific pretraining consistently outperforms general-purpose initialization: word embeddings trained on legal corpora (Law2Vec) perform better than Google News vectors, and fine-tuned LegalBERT outperforms fine-tuned BERT. Cross-jurisdictional transfer experiments demonstrate that models trained on one jurisdiction can be successfully adapted to another with relatively small amounts of in-domain data, a practically important finding for jurisdictions where annotated legal data is scarce. The neural models' prediction errors on subjective label pairs closely mirror the patterns of human disagreement observed in the IAA study, providing a coherent account of the irreducible difficulty of the task.

The paper concludes that Hierarchical BiLSTM architectures represent a strong and robust approach to rhetorical role labeling, offering substantial improvements over prior CRF-based methods that relied on manually engineered features. The authors acknowledge that the transparency and explainability limitations of neural models present a practical obstacle to deployment in legal settings, where accountability requirements are high. Future research directions include multi-label classification to handle inherently ambiguous sentences, application of labeled data to downstream tasks such as summarization and document similarity, and extension of the evaluation to non-Common Law legal systems, which exhibit structurally different document conventions.

## 03_06 — CoTHSSum: Structured long-document summarization via chain-of-thought reasoning and hierarchical segmentation

**Authors:** Xiaoyong Chen, Zhiqiang Chen, Shi Cheng  
**Journal:** Journal of King Saud University – Computer and Information Sciences (2025), vol. 37, 40  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s44443-025-00041-2

Chen, Chen, and Cheng present CoTHSSum, a novel framework for the summarization of long documents that combines hierarchical input segmentation with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting to guide large language models (LLMs) through structured, multi-step reasoning. The work addresses two fundamental limitations that persist in LLM-based summarization of long texts: the tendency to produce factually inaccurate or hallucinated content when the model is forced to compress a large amount of information in a single generative step, and the omission of critical content from document segments that fall beyond the model's effective attention window. Both failure modes are particularly consequential in high-stakes domains such as law, biomedicine, and policy, where summary fidelity is not merely a quality metric but a prerequisite for practical usability.

The CoTHSSum framework operates through a two-level hierarchical process. At the lower level, the input document is decomposed into semantically coherent segments, each of which is independently summarized using a CoT prompting strategy that elicits intermediate reasoning steps — such as key point identification, claim extraction, and logical connection — before producing a segment-level sub-summary. At the higher level, the segment summaries are aggregated and refined through a structure-guided decoding mechanism that preserves the logical flow of the original document while producing a coherent final summary. This architecture ensures that each segment receives dedicated attention and that the global narrative structure is maintained across the compositional process, mitigating the incoherence that typically arises from naive chunking approaches.

The framework is evaluated across five diverse benchmark datasets spanning scientific (ArXiv, PubMed), governmental (GovReport), literary (BookSum), and legal (CAILsfzy) domains, using multiple strong LLM backbones including Qwen, LLaMA, and Phi. Performance is assessed across four complementary metrics — ROUGE, BLEU, BERTScore, and FactCC (a factual consistency measure) — providing a comprehensive view of summary quality that goes beyond surface-level lexical overlap. CoTHSSum consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on all five datasets and across all four metrics, with the legal domain results confirming the framework's applicability to structured, domain-specific long documents.

Ablation studies confirm that both the hierarchical segmentation and the CoT prompting components make independent and complementary contributions to overall performance. Removing either component leads to measurable performance degradation across all metrics, validating the design hypothesis that structured decomposition and explicit reasoning guidance are synergistic rather than redundant strategies. Human evaluation further corroborates the automatic metrics, with CoTHSSum summaries rated higher in factual consistency, completeness, fluency, and structural clarity compared to summaries generated by single-pass LLM baselines.

The authors acknowledge that the framework introduces computational overhead relative to direct LLM summarization, as each segment is processed independently before final assembly. This trade-off is identified as the primary limitation of the approach, alongside questions of domain adaptability when the segmentation heuristics are applied to highly heterogeneous document types. Future work is planned to address these limitations through the development of adaptive segmentation algorithms, automatic CoT prompt generation, and extension of the framework to cross-lingual and multimodal summarization tasks. The paper makes a methodologically significant contribution by demonstrating that combining explicit reasoning with hierarchical structure provides a principled and scalable solution to the long-document summarization problem.

## 03_07 — Legal sentence boundary detection using hybrid deep learning and statistical models

**Authors:** Reshma Sheik, Sneha Rao Ganta, S. Jaya Nirmala  
**Journal:** Artificial Intelligence and Law (2025), vol. 33, pp. 519–549  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10506-024-09394-x

Sheik, Ganta, and Nirmala investigate the problem of sentence boundary detection (SBD) in legal texts, arguing that accurate identification of sentence boundaries is a foundational preprocessing step whose quality has cascading effects on all downstream NLP applications, including legal argument mining, text summarization, rhetorical role segmentation, and information retrieval. Legal texts present substantially greater challenges for SBD than general-domain text due to their characteristic use of lengthy and structurally complex sentences, extensive lists, abbreviations, numerical citations, and domain-specific punctuation conventions. The ambiguity of period-like delimiters — which may indicate sentence endings, abbreviations, citations, or numerical values depending on context — is identified as the principal source of difficulty, motivating the development of context-aware classification models rather than rule-based heuristics.

The proposed approach treats SBD as a binary sequence classification problem, in which each delimiter token is classified as either a true sentence boundary or a non-boundary. The input to the classifier consists of the delimiter and its surrounding context, enabling the model to exploit both local character patterns and broader syntactic context. A range of deep learning models is evaluated, including standard LSTM and CNN architectures as well as domain-specific transformer models — LegalBERT and CaseLawBERT — pretrained on large legal corpora. These models are compared against a state-of-the-art statistical baseline in the form of a Conditional Random Field (CRF) model with domain-specific handcrafted features. After considering the trade-offs among model size, F1-score, and inference time, the CNN model is identified as the optimal deep learning candidate, offering the best balance of performance and computational efficiency.

A hybrid CNN+CRF architecture is then developed to combine the complementary strengths of the two model families. The CNN extracts distributed feature representations that capture local contextual patterns around delimiters, while the CRF layer uses these features as input to optimize label sequences globally, enforcing consistency between adjacent classification decisions. The hybrid model achieves an F1-score improvement of over 4% compared to either component model individually, demonstrating that the integration is not merely additive but synergistic — the CRF benefits from the richer feature representations provided by the CNN, while the CNN's output is regularized by the CRF's sequential modeling constraints.

Generalizability of the hybrid model is assessed through an out-of-domain evaluation on an Indian legal text corpus, where the model is applied without retraining. The results demonstrate that the hybrid architecture maintains its superiority over standard open-source SBD toolkits even on this unseen domain, which differs substantially from the English legal corpora used for training. This finding is theoretically significant: it suggests that the contextual, delimiter-centric feature representation learned by the CNN captures properties of legal sentence structure that are shared across jurisdictions and legal systems, rather than being narrowly tailored to the training corpus.

The authors identify several directions for future research, including the systematic evaluation of downstream task performance improvements attributable to enhanced SBD, the integration of rule-based components to handle rare error patterns identified through error analysis, and the development of multilingual SBD models capable of handling legal texts in languages that lack explicit sentence delimiters, such as Thai. The paper makes a clear and practically impactful contribution to legal NLP preprocessing by establishing the hybrid CNN+CRF architecture as a state-of-the-art approach for legal SBD, and by providing an open-source implementation that enables the research community to build upon and extend the proposed method.

## 03_08 — A Multi-Modal Approach to Digital Document Stream Segmentation for Title Insurance Domain

**Authors:** Abhijit Guha, Abdulrahman Alahmadi, Debabrata Samanta, Mohammad Zubair Khan, Ahmed H. Alahmadi  
**Journal:** IEEE Access (2022), vol. 10, pp. 11341–11352  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1109/ACCESS.2022.3144185

Guha et al. present a multi-modal document stream segmentation (DSS) system designed specifically for the Title Insurance (TI) industry, where the automated processing of heterogeneous batches of scanned legal and financial documents is a core operational requirement. The problem of DSS — identifying the boundaries between distinct documents within a continuous page stream — is framed as a binary classification task: each page is labeled as either the First Page (FP) of a new document or an Other Page (OP) belonging to the current document. The practical motivation is compelling: in TI workflows, document packages comprising mortgage deeds, title searches, property surveys, and legal certificates arrive as undifferentiated page sequences, and any misclassification in the segmentation stage propagates as errors through all subsequent automated processing modules, including document classification and information extraction. Prior state-of-the-art models achieved at most 86.7% accuracy on the proprietary TI dataset, well below the operational threshold required for production deployment.

The proposed architecture, which the authors refer to as a multi-modal binary classification network, simultaneously processes two modalities of each document page: the visual appearance of the page image, extracted using a VGG16 convolutional neural network pretrained on ImageNet; and the textual content of the page, encoded using a Legal-BERTbase model that leverages domain-specific pretraining on legal corpora. Both feature vectors are independently computed and subsequently fused through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) classifier, which produces the binary FP/OP prediction. The key design choice is the replacement of the general-purpose text encoders used in prior DSS work with Legal-BERTbase, exploiting the semantic specialization of transformer models pretrained on legal documents for a task that is predominantly textual in nature.

The empirical evaluation is conducted on a proprietary dataset sourced from a reputed TI company's production archive, comprising two temporal splits (CP2020 and SP2020) to assess both within-sample and cross-sample performance. The multi-modal model achieves F1-scores of 97.37% and 97.15% on the two archive sets, substantially exceeding the performance of prior approaches. A controlled ablation confirms the relative contribution of each modality: a text-only model using Legal-BERTbase already achieves strong performance, while the addition of the image modality provides a further improvement of approximately 1% in F1-score. Although this marginal gain appears modest in absolute terms, the authors demonstrate that in a high-volume production environment processing thousands of documents daily, even a 1% F1 improvement translates to a 2.32% increase in the Straight Through Pass rate — the proportion of documents processed without human intervention — representing significant operational value.

The finding that textual features dominate the model's predictive performance, with visual features providing a supplementary benefit, carries theoretical implications for the design of multi-modal document processing systems. It suggests that for document types in which textual content is the primary carrier of discriminative information — as is typical in legal documents — the investment in a high-quality language model encoder is more impactful than architectural innovations in the image processing pathway. The success of Legal-BERTbase in particular underscores the value of domain-specific pretraining even for tasks that do not involve explicit semantic understanding of text content, such as document boundary classification.

The paper concludes by noting that further performance gains may be achievable by exploiting page-specific visual features — such as font size, margin configuration, logo placement, and document titles — through sequence-aware architectures such as recurrent neural networks, which can model the temporal ordering of pages within a stream. Future research is planned to explore this direction, in addition to investigating the generalizability of the proposed approach to other document-intensive industries beyond TI. The work establishes a strong empirical baseline for multi-modal DSS in the legal domain and provides a practically deployed model whose industrial validation lends its results particular credibility.

## 03_09 — A domain-specific cross-lingual semantic alignment learning model for low-resource languages

**Authors:** Yurong Wang, Min Lin, Qitu Hu, Shuangcheng Bai, Yanling Li, Longjie Bao  
**Journal:** Neural Networks (2026), vol. 194, 108114  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neunet.2025.108114

Wang et al. propose CLWKD, a cross-lingual mapping and knowledge distillation framework designed to address the challenge of domain-specific semantic alignment for low-resource agglutinative languages. The work is motivated by the growing need for NLP capabilities in specialized domains — including law, medicine, and education — in the context of China's multilingual ethnic minorities, whose languages (such as Mongolian, Tibetan, Kazakh, and Uyghur) are severely underrepresented in existing pretrained language models. Standard cross-lingual alignment approaches face three compounding obstacles in this setting: the scarcity of domain-specific parallel corpora for low-resource language pairs; semantic space heterogeneity arising from significant syntactic and morphological differences between source and target languages; and the complex agglutinative morphology of target languages, which exacerbates out-of-vocabulary problems and impedes standard tokenization strategies.

CLWKD addresses these challenges through a two-stage framework. In the first stage, multi-granularity semantic alignment mapping matrices are learned at the token, word, and sentence levels from general-domain parallel corpora. This multi-granularity strategy is theoretically justified by the authors' argument that word-level and sentence-level alignment are mutually reinforcing processes in low-resource settings, and that relying solely on either level may lead to semantic drift or grammatical inconsistency, particularly for structurally divergent language pairs. To stabilize the adversarial mapping training, which is known to be sensitive to initialization and prone to oscillation, the authors introduce generator pretraining combined with high-confidence word and sentence pair selection as auxiliary supervision signals.

In the second stage, the general-domain mapping matrices learned in stage one serve as a bridge for domain-specific knowledge distillation. A high-resource language pretrained model acts as a teacher network, and domain-specific knowledge is transferred to a multilingual student model via a distillation objective augmented by the mapping matrices. This design reduces the reliance on large-scale domain-specific parallel corpora by leveraging the structural knowledge encoded in the general-domain mapping as a form of inductive bias that guides domain adaptation. Additional techniques — including multilingual embedding sharing and morphological segmentation strategies — are incorporated to specifically address the out-of-vocabulary and data sparsity challenges posed by agglutinative morphology.

To limit the parameter footprint of the alignment components while preserving representational capacity, CLWKD employs a parameter recycling and embedding bottleneck design, making the framework more suitable for deployment in resource-constrained settings. Experiments across medical, legal, and educational domains on Mongolian-Chinese and Korean-Chinese language pairs validate the effectiveness of CLWKD in three cross-lingual tasks — demonstrating that the framework generalizes across both domain types and language pairs with distinct structural properties.

Based on the abstract and the experimental findings reported, the paper demonstrates that CLWKD achieves consistent improvements over competitive cross-lingual baselines, particularly in the most low-resource and morphologically complex language configurations, and establishes multi-granularity alignment as a principled strategy for domain-specific cross-lingual transfer in underrepresented language settings.
