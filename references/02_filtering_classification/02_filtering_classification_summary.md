# 02 — Filtering and Classification of Legal Documents: Article Summaries

## 02_01 — LegalVis: Exploring and Inferring Precedent Citations in Legal Documents

**Authors:** Lucas E. Resck, Jean R. Ponciano, Luis Gustavo Nonato, Jorge Poco  
**Journal:** IEEE Transactions on Visualization and Computer Graphics (2023), vol. 29, no. 6, pp. 3105–3120  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1109/TVCG.2022.3152450

Resck et al. introduce LegalVis, a web-based visual analytics system designed to assist legal professionals in identifying and analyzing both explicit and implicit citations to binding precedents (BPs) within the decisions of the Brazilian Supreme Court (STF). The motivation for this work arises from the scale and complexity of the STF's document corpus — comprising over one million decisions issued between 2011 and 2020 — and the institutional importance of binding precedents, which represent authoritative legal interpretations that all lower courts and executive bodies are constitutionally required to follow. Traditional keyword-based search approaches are insufficient for detecting implicit citations, i.e., cases where a precedent is substantively referenced without explicit textual mention, motivating the use of a machine learning classification framework.

The proposed system models the detection of potential (non-explicit) precedent citations as a supervised classification problem. A machine learning model is trained to predict whether a given legal document or paragraph should be associated with a particular binding precedent, achieving an accuracy of 96%. To address the interpretability requirements of legal professionals — who cannot rely solely on a black-box prediction score — LegalVis integrates an interpretability mechanism that explains each classification decision by highlighting which paragraphs within a document are most likely to contain a relevant citation. This combination of predictive accuracy and post-hoc explainability is presented as a core design principle of the system.

The interface of LegalVis is organized around three interactive, linked visual components. The first provides an overview of the corpus, enabling the exploration of temporal patterns in citation behavior across the 58 existing binding precedents. The second component supports document filtering and topical grouping, allowing users to narrow down the corpus to sets of documents that are thematically relevant to a specific precedent. The third component presents the full text of individual documents with visual annotations that highlight paragraphs where citation probability is high, supporting close reading and expert validation of the model's output.

Evaluation of the system was conducted through both quantitative assessment of the classification model and qualitative user studies. Two usage scenarios were developed to illustrate the practical utility of LegalVis for legal research tasks, such as auditing whether certain decisions should have cited a given precedent or identifying trends in how specific precedents are applied over time. Feedback from six domain experts — who were not involved in the system's development — confirmed the usefulness and effectiveness of LegalVis in supporting these tasks.

The paper constitutes a significant contribution at the intersection of legal NLP and visual analytics. It demonstrates that combining a high-accuracy classification model with an interpretability layer and an intuitive visual interface yields a system that is not only technically robust but also practically usable by legal professionals. The approach is particularly relevant to jurisdictions with large, digitized legal corpora and institutional mechanisms such as binding precedents, and it establishes a methodological template for similar legal analytics applications in other legal systems.

## 02_02 — Natural Language Processing for the Legal Domain: A Survey of Tasks, Datasets, Models, and Challenges

**Authors:** Farid Ariai, Joel Mackenzie, Gianluca Demartini  
**Journal:** ACM Computing Surveys (2025), vol. 58, no. 6, Article 163  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3777009

Ariai, Mackenzie, and Demartini present a comprehensive systematic survey of natural language processing (NLP) research applied to the legal domain, conducted following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) framework. The survey reviews 154 studies, with 131 retained after manual filtering, and covers a broad spectrum of tasks, datasets, models, and open research challenges. The authors motivate the survey by observing that advances in transformer-based architectures and large language models (LLMs) have significantly expanded the scope and performance of legal NLP systems, automating tasks such as document summarization, judgment prediction, named entity recognition, and argument mining — yet no unified and rigorous synthesis of this landscape existed prior to their work.

The survey systematically characterizes the unique challenges that distinguish legal text from general-domain text, including extensive document lengths, highly specialized and jurisdiction-dependent vocabulary, complex argumentation structures, and the scarcity of openly available annotated legal datasets. These features render many general-purpose NLP tools inadequate when applied to legal materials without domain-specific adaptation. The authors provide an organized taxonomy of legal NLP tasks — covering Legal Document Summarization (LDS), Legal Named Entity Recognition (NER), Legal Question Answering (LQA), Legal Judgment Prediction (LJP), Legal Text Classification (LTC), and Legal Argument Mining (LAM) — and review the state of the art within each category.

A central finding of the survey is that research activity is unevenly distributed across tasks: LDS and LQA have attracted the most attention, while NER and legal corpus construction remain comparatively underexplored. Across all tasks, there is a notable concentration of work on English-language and Chinese-language legal systems, with limited representation of other jurisdictions. The survey also documents the evolution of modelling approaches from early rule-based and feature-engineered systems to contemporary fine-tuned transformer models and LLMs, noting that domain-specific pretraining — as exemplified by models such as LEGAL-BERT and AraLegal-BERT — consistently yields performance gains over general-purpose alternatives.

The survey identifies sixteen open research challenges, which collectively highlight the need for more robust, interpretable, and fair legal NLP systems. Key challenges include: the development of domain-specific fine-tuning strategies that can leverage limited annotated data; the detection and mitigation of bias embedded in legal datasets and model outputs; the improvement of explainability mechanisms to support accountability in AI-assisted legal decision-making; and the extension of legal NLP capabilities to multilingual and low-resource settings. The authors also identify the integration of ontology-based reasoning as a promising avenue for improving legal inference beyond the pattern-matching capabilities of current neural models.

By consolidating and synthesizing a decade of research across multiple legal NLP tasks and modelling paradigms, this survey provides an indispensable reference for researchers entering or contributing to the field. Its systematic methodology, combined with the identification of concrete open problems, positions it as both a retrospective account of progress and a forward-looking roadmap for future research. The work directly supports the design of systems that combine multiple legal NLP capabilities — such as document classification, entity extraction, and judgment prediction — within end-to-end processing pipelines.

## 02_03 — A systematical procedure to extracting legal entities from Indonesian judicial decisions

**Authors:** Eka Qadri Nuranti, Naili Suri Intizhami, Evi Yulianti, A. Muh. Iqbal Latief, Osama Iyad Al Ghozy  
**Journal:** MethodsX (2025), vol. 15, Article 103610  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mex.2025.103610

Nuranti et al. describe a systematic end-to-end methodology for constructing an annotated corpus of Indonesian judicial decisions for the purpose of named entity recognition (NER), addressing a recognized gap in legal NLP resources for Bahasa Indonesia. The work is framed as a methodological contribution — providing a reproducible pipeline rather than presenting a trained model — and targets theft case decisions sourced from the official website of the Supreme Court of the Republic of Indonesia. The corpus spans three levels of the judicial hierarchy: first instance (2,478 files), appeal (147 files), and cassation (62 files), yielding a total of 2,687 annotated documents. This multi-tier coverage is a distinctive feature of the dataset, as prior Indonesian legal NLP resources have generally been limited to first-instance decisions and smaller sample sizes.

The data collection process begins with automated web scraping of publicly available judicial decisions, followed by manual filtering for relevance and document completeness. Annotation is performed using the Label Studio platform by three independent annotators, who assign labels to over 50 entity types — including defendants, judges, legal articles, charges, and verdict decisions — using a hierarchical BIO (Beginning-Inside-Outside) tagging scheme. The use of a hierarchical entity structure allows the annotation to capture the compositional nature of legal entities, where broader categories may subsume more specific sub-entities. Inter-annotator agreement is evaluated using Fleiss' Kappa, yielding an average score of 0.705 across all judicial levels, which falls within the "Good" agreement range and confirms the reliability of the annotation process.

The granularity and reproducibility of the methodology constitute the primary scholarly contributions of the paper. By documenting each step of the pipeline — from scraping and filtering through annotation, quality control, and format conversion — the authors provide a template that can be adapted to other case types, legal domains, or jurisdictions with similar document accessibility. The methodological choices are explicitly justified with reference to prior Indonesian legal NLP work, and the limitations of those earlier efforts (small scale, single-level coverage, narrow entity schemas) are directly addressed through the design of the present pipeline.

The resulting corpus, named IDTheftCase-JudgmentCorpus, represents a substantial expansion of available resources for Indonesian legal NLP. Its scale — nearly 2,700 documents across three judicial levels — and its rich entity schema make it particularly well-suited for training and evaluating neural NER models, including architectures based on BERT and its multilingual or domain-specific variants. The authors note that the annotation scheme's breadth (>50 entity types) enables the corpus to support not only NER but also downstream tasks such as legal information extraction, classification, and large-scale judicial analysis.

The paper acknowledges that the corpus is currently restricted to theft cases, which limits its immediate generalizability to other areas of Indonesian criminal or civil law. Nevertheless, the authors argue that thematic consistency is a deliberate design choice that reduces inter-document variability and facilitates more controlled model training. Future work is expected to extend the pipeline to other case categories and potentially to other jurisdictions, contributing to the broader goal of building AI-ready legal corpora for low-resource linguistic settings, where the combination of language scarcity and specialized domain vocabulary presents particular challenges for standard NLP approaches.
