## 01_01 — An entity-centric approach to manage court judgments based on Natural Language Processing

**Authors:** Valerio Bellandi, Christian Bernasconi, Fausto Lodi, Matteo Palmonari, Riccardo Pozzi, Marco Ripamonti, Stefano Siccardi  
**Journal:** Computer Law & Security Review (2024), vol. 52, 105904  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clsr.2024.105904

Bellandi et al. present an entity-centric infrastructure for the automated management and annotation of legal documents, with a particular focus on Italian court judgments. The motivation for this work stems from the high practical value that structured information extraction from legal texts offers to a diverse range of stakeholders — including judges, lawyers, legal scholars, and public administrators — who require efficient access to entities such as persons, organizations, locations, dates, and monetary values embedded within lengthy and complex legal documents. The authors position their contribution at the intersection of data management, NLP, and machine learning, arguing that the combination of these disciplines is necessary to handle the scarcity of annotated legal data and the heterogeneity of document structures.

The proposed framework organizes a repository of court judgments and subjects them to an iterative annotation pipeline. This pipeline relies on a set of NLP services that combine complementary approaches: machine learning models and syntactic rule-based methods are used in tandem to maximize coverage and precision. A distinctive design choice is the adoption of a human-in-the-loop strategy, in which legal professionals validate and correct the output of the automated annotation algorithms. This approach is motivated by both the inherent ambiguity of legal language and the institutional requirements of Italian courts, where annotation decisions must align with established organizational processes.

The framework was designed with long-term sustainability in mind. Rather than targeting a single downstream task, it was conceived as a multi-purpose infrastructure capable of supporting legal case retrieval, trend analysis, and entity-based faceted search, among other applications. The co-design methodology — involving a scientific team, institutional bodies, and implementation companies — reflects the practical nature of the project and its orientation toward eventual national-scale deployment.

Experimental results reported in the paper demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed approach, particularly with respect to low-resource annotation methods. The authors evaluate several strategies for combining limited annotated data with both machine learning and rule-based NLP services, reporting encouraging performance metrics that support the viability of the solution even in data-scarce settings. The human-in-the-loop component is identified as essential for maintaining quality and institutional alignment during the annotation process.

The authors conclude that the system can be extended to other languages and jurisdictions with relatively modest adjustments — primarily replacing the language-specific NLP service modules and revising the Entity Registry metamodel to accommodate country-specific conventions. Future directions include incorporating feedback learning from user interactions to refine the annotation process over time. Overall, this work constitutes a comprehensive and practically grounded framework for entity-level processing of legal documents, with direct applicability to scenarios where structured information extraction is required at scale.

## 01_02 — Information extraction framework to build legislation network

**Authors:** Neda Sakhaee, Mark C. Wilson  
**Journal:** Artificial Intelligence and Law (2021), vol. 29, pp. 35–58  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10506-020-09263-3

Sakhaee and Wilson propose an information extraction framework designed to construct a dynamic legislation network from large-scale legal corpora. The core contribution of the work is methodological: rather than relying on supervised machine learning approaches — which require annotated training data and additional computational overhead — the authors adopt a rule-based information extraction strategy that identifies and processes specific textual expressions in legal documents to infer citation relationships. The resulting network represents legislation as a dynamic graph in which nodes correspond to individual Acts and directed edges encode cross-references between them, allowing researchers to study how legislative texts are interconnected and how those connections evolve over time.

The practical scope of the framework is illustrated through a large-scale case study covering the entire New Zealand legislation corpus, comprising 23,870 PDF files of Acts — including historical, expired, repealed, and consolidated versions as of September 2018. A significant practical challenge addressed in this work is the poor quality of the source documents: approximately 87% of the files are in scanned image format, requiring robust pre-processing pipelines before text-based extraction can take place. The authors introduce improvements to approximate string matching techniques, reporting precision and recall values exceeding 98%, which they argue are substantially higher than those achieved by prior comparable systems.

A central theoretical claim of the paper is that time is an essential attribute for understanding and analyzing legislation. The authors emphasize the importance of studying legislation not as a static collection of documents but as a dynamic network that evolves through the enactment, amendment, and repeal of Acts. This temporal perspective enables analyses that are unavailable when treating legal corpora in isolation, such as tracing the propagation of legislative intent across different historical periods or identifying influential Acts based on their citation centrality.

Beyond the domain of law, the authors argue that the proposed framework is generalizable to any knowledge domain in which documented relationships between artifacts can be extracted from large textual corpora. The methodology does not presuppose legal domain expertise and can be adapted to other jurisdictions by modifying the extraction patterns to reflect jurisdiction-specific conventions for legislative citation and cross-referencing. This generalizability is presented as a key advantage over supervised learning approaches that are tightly coupled to particular annotated datasets.

The paper concludes by acknowledging several avenues for future research, including the mathematical modelling of network growth and evolution, the study of betweenness centrality and the impact of removing hub nodes, and comparative analyses across the legislation networks of different jurisdictions. The work makes a substantive contribution to the intersection of computational legal analysis and network science, offering a replicable, high-precision methodology for transforming unstructured legal corpora into structured, queryable graph representations.

## 01_03 — Design of Knowledge Graph Retrieval System for Legal and Regulatory Framework of Multilevel Latent Semantic Indexing

**Authors:** Guicun Zhu, Meihui Hao, Changlong Zheng, Linlin Wang  
**Journal:** Computational Intelligence and Neuroscience (2022), Article ID 6781043  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1155/2022/6781043

Zhu et al. present a knowledge graph retrieval system designed for the legal and public prosecutorial domain in China, combining latent semantic analysis (LSA), named entity recognition (NER), and graph database technology to support intelligent legal information management. The work is motivated by the growing volume of judicial cases handled by Chinese courts — where individual judges may process over 100 cases per year — and by the national policy initiative known as "Smart Court," which seeks to integrate artificial intelligence into the judicial system. The authors argue that domain-specific knowledge graphs offer a practical mechanism for representing the deep, structured knowledge inherent to legal documents, providing capabilities for intelligent querying, case recommendation, and knowledge management that surpass traditional keyword-based retrieval.

The system architecture is built around three core components. First, a knowledge graph is constructed using ontology technology and stored in a Neo4j graph database, enabling flexible and semantically rich representation of legal entities and their relationships. Second, a NER module based on a bidirectional long short-term memory network combined with a conditional random field (bi-LSTM-CRF) is employed to extract entities such as defendants, charges, judicial organizations, and legal articles from raw legal text. Third, a latent semantic indexing mechanism, implemented via truncated singular value decomposition, is applied to build a latent semantic space that supports similarity-based retrieval and case recommendation.

The theoretical contribution of the paper lies in the authors' analysis of the relationship between the dimensions of the latent semantic space and the granularity of linguistic concepts. The authors observe that dimensions corresponding to large singular values capture broadly shared linguistic patterns (i.e., "commonality"), while dimensions associated with small singular values capture domain-specific or idiosyncratic patterns (i.e., "personality"). This analysis provides a principled basis for understanding how LSA can be tuned to balance general semantic coverage with domain-specific precision — an important consideration when applying the method to highly specialized legal text.

The NER component of the system introduces a multichannel input strategy in which text representations, word vector embeddings, and knowledge entity vectors are fused to enrich the contextual information available to the bi-LSTM-CRF model. This design allows the model to leverage both the linguistic context of a token and its semantic relationship to known legal entities, resulting in improvements in named entity recognition accuracy over single-channel baselines. The experimental results reported in the paper confirm that the proposed LSTM-entity-context method achieves superior text semantic representation compared to alternative approaches, with particular gains observed in crime prediction tasks across different case types.

The paper concludes by validating the accuracy and practical feasibility of the integrated system through prototype implementation. The system supports online import of new entity nodes and relationships, natural language querying, and similar case recommendation, thereby diversifying and enriching the functionality available to legal professionals. Future work is expected to focus on expanding the ontology to cover additional legal domains, refining the knowledge graph maintenance pipeline for real-time updates, and improving the interpretability of the recommendation mechanism in alignment with the transparency requirements of judicial applications.
